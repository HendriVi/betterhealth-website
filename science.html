The science behind BetterHealth

Mental health is the state of a living system.

BetterHealth starts from a simple clinical idea: symptoms are real experiences, and they emerge from the interaction between brain, body, environment, behavior and time. The work is to understand which systems are involved, which signals are clinically useful, and which interventions have the best chance of changing the person’s actual life.

The central premise

A diagnosis gives structure. A formulation explains the pattern.

Conventional diagnosis remains important. It creates a shared clinical language, supports safety decisions and protects against vague wellness thinking. But a diagnosis often describes a symptom cluster rather than the full biological and psychological architecture of the case.

Two people can both meet criteria for depression and yet live inside very different systems. One may be sleep deprived, inflamed and metabolically unstable. Another may live in a long-standing threat prediction shaped by trauma and avoidance. A third may have low recovery capacity after chronic overload, endocrine transition or repeated concussion. The treatment should follow the pattern, not only the label.

Each scientific layer answers a different clinical question. The value is not in naming more systems. The value is in knowing which system may be maintaining the current state, what would suggest it, what can be measured and what can be changed.

P
Layer 01

Prediction shapes perception.

The brain predicts threat, safety, effort and control. A changed body state can make the same world feel more dangerous, exhausting or impossible to act in.

What may suggest it

Panic, avoidance, interoceptive fear, rumination, trauma history or a constant sense that the body is unsafe.

What can be measured

Clinical interview, avoidance map, symptom scores, sleep and autonomic context.

What treatment can target

Psychotherapy, exposure design, HRV work, sleep repair and behavior experiments that update prediction.

The clinical algorithm

Symptom → System → Signal → Strategy.

The algorithm is a structured clinical reasoning tool. It does not replace diagnosis, psychotherapy or medical judgment. It helps rank what may be maintaining the current state and what should be measured first.

Step 01

Clinical phenotype

Symptoms, diagnosis, severity, chronology, function and risk are defined before biological interpretation begins.

Step 02

System hypothesis

The case is mapped across sleep, autonomic, metabolic, inflammatory, endocrine, bioenergetic, neurocognitive and psychological systems.

Step 03

Useful signals

Testing is chosen when it can change clinical interpretation, treatment choice, safety, urgency or monitoring.

Step 04

Measured strategy

Psychotherapy, physiology, training, sleep, nutrition and selected modalities are matched to the dominant systems and retested over time.

Why psychotherapy remains central

The biology matters because experience is embodied. Therapy matters because prediction changes through experience.

BetterHealth is not built around testing alone. The patient still needs meaning, relationship, behavior change and emotional learning. Psychotherapy is where prediction, avoidance, threat learning, identity, grief, trauma and agency are worked with directly.

The difference is that therapy is supported by a wider map. Sleep debt, metabolic instability, autonomic hyperarousal or inflammatory load can make the psychological work harder. When those layers are identified and treated, psychotherapy can become more precise, more tolerable and more useful in the person’s real life.

Selected scientific lineages

The model is an integration, not a single-paper claim.

The BetterHealth framework is shaped by precision psychiatry, RDoC and dimensional models, predictive processing and active inference, allostatic load, mitochondrial psychobiology, evolutionary medicine, metabolic psychiatry and measurement-based care.

Precision psychiatry

Biomarker-informed stratification, RDoC and HiTOP help move from broad labels toward clinically useful dimensions.

Predictive brain

Predictive processing and active inference explain how body state can shape perception, threat, effort and control.

Stress biology

Allostatic load models explain how long-term adaptation creates cost across multiple physiological systems.

Energy and mismatch

Mitochondrial biology, metabolic psychiatry and evolutionary medicine connect modern load with recovery capacity.

Clinical boundaries

Scientific seriousness also means restraint.

Biomarkers support clinical formulation. They do not replace diagnosis, risk assessment, psychotherapy, medical examination or specialist referral. The purpose is to improve the quality of questions and the fit of the plan, while staying honest about what is established, what is promising and what remains uncertain.
